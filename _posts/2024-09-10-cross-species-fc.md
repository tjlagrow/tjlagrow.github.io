One of the "Valley of Death" problems in neuroscience is the Translation Gap. We often cure diseases in mice, only for the treatments to fail in human clinical trials. A major culprit is the assumption of a 1:1 mapping between mouse and human brain networks that doesn't fully exist.

My compilation, **"Functional Connectivity of the Brain Across Rodents and Humans"** (LaGrow, Xu, et al., 2022), addresses this by systematically mapping the homologous functional networks across species.

### The "Rosetta Stone" of fMRI
We utilized high-field fMRI (9.4T for rodents, 7T/3T for humans) to compare resting-state networks (RSNs) side-by-side.

### Key Homologies & Divergences
1.  **Default Mode Network (DMN):**
    *   *Human:* Robust, well-defined, and central to resting state.
    *   *Rodent:* A proto-DMN exists (anterior cingulate, retrosplenial cortex), but its coupling strength and topology differ significantly. It's less "dominant" than in humans.
    
2.  **The Global Signal:**
    *   In humans, the Global Signal (GS) is often regressed out as noise.
    *   In rodents, the GS contains significant neural information related to arousal and vascular dynamics. Treating it as noise in mice can remove the very signal of interest.

3.  **Dynamic Conservation:**
    *   Despite these static differences, we found that **Quasi-Periodic Patterns (QPPs)** are remarkably conserved. The infraslow propagation of activity from lateral to medial regions appears to be a fundamental principle of mammalian brain organization, preserved across 70 million years of evolution.

### Implications for Drug Discovery
Understanding these distinctions is vital. If a drug targets the DMN in a mouse model, but the mouse DMN has different receptor density or connectivity than the human DMN, the trial is doomed to fail. Our framework provides a "lookup table" to help researchers contextualize rodent fMRI findings before jumping to human trials.
